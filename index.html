Welcome to the Group Ethics Questionnaire

This questionnaire is designed to be completed as a group. It serves a dual purpose: it is both your formal ethics application for the Overseas Practice Engagement and a structured opportunity to think carefully about two things that are deeply connected — ethics and research design.

The questions that follow build on what we discussed last week. Work through each section together, drawing on your group's collective knowledge of your project site, your participants, and the methods you plan to use. Disagreement is productive — if a question sparks debate within the group, that is exactly the kind of reflection this process is meant to encourage.

Ethics as an Ongoing Process

Before
Planning and preparation — anticipating ethical challenges before you enter the field.
During
Responsive ethical decision-making — adapting in real time as situations unfold.
After
Ethical data management and reporting — handling what you have gathered with care.
Throughout
Reflection and adaptation — treating ethics not as a hurdle but as a continuous practice.

Personal Data · Step 3 of 11

Personal Data

The legal definition of Personal Data is any information relating to an identifiable person who could directly or indirectly be identified from that information. It includes Personal Data that you are not using as research data — e.g. personal data you are collecting simply to contact participants. It is data that a motivated intruder or analyst (including you) could use to identify someone. This includes if someone could potentially identify individuals from an interview transcript, or from a subsequent report.

Protected characteristics and ethical risk

Collecting data on protected characteristics (e.g. race, religion, sexual orientation, health) can increase a project's ethical risk, even when data is fully anonymised. While anonymised data may fall outside data protection regulations, research ethics still consider broader risks. These include the potential for re-identification through combining variables (deductive disclosure), harm or stigma to specific groups, the inherently sensitive nature of such data, and the risk of misuse through linkage with other datasets. For these reasons, studies involving protected characteristics are often classified as higher risk and may require additional safeguards.

To determine if your project is high-risk, ask:
• Are the participants vulnerable? (e.g., children, vulnerable people).
• Could findings be used to discriminate against a group?
• Is the data granular enough to potentially identify individuals?

Section 10 · Step 10 of 11

Risk to the Research Team

Working in potentially unsafe environments (e.g. overseas research where the FCO has advised against all travel); lone working such as at night in non-public places where there are other risk elements to consider (for example, the study may be looking at domestic abuse); engaging with groups or behaviour that could result in harm.

The Social Research Association highlights these potential risks to researchers: physical threat or abuse; psychological trauma, as a result of actual or threatened violence or the nature of what is disclosed during the interaction; being in a compromising situation, in which there might be accusations of improper behaviour; increased exposure to risks of everyday life and social interaction, such as road accidents and infectious illness; causing psychological or physical harm to others.
